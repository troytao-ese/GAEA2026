Background & Vision

The Environmental Reckoning of AI

Terawatt-hours of projected global data centre electricity consumption in 2026 (IEA estimate)
~500 mL
Water consumed per 10–50 prompts in a ChatGPT-scale model — a largely invisible "water footprint"
2×
Rate at which large-model training energy demand has doubled relative to hardware efficiency gains

The explosion of large language models and foundation AI systems has transformed data centres into one of the fastest-growing sources of energy demand on the planet. The environmental costs — carbon, water, and thermal — are substantial and under-scrutinised. Yet the very tools driving this demand also offer transformative potential for environmental monitoring, climate modelling, and resource optimisation.

"GAEA inverts the usual framing. Rather than asking how AI can help the environment, we begin by asking: what does AI cost the environment — and how do we fix that?"

Track A of the GAEA forum brings together the world's foremost authorities on sustainable computing to address the full lifecycle environmental impact of AI systems: from chip fabrication and model training, through inference at scale, to hardware end-of-life and circular economy. The session will produce concrete outputs — a science-policy brief and an industry declaration — aimed at establishing internationally harmonised standards for AI energy and environmental reporting.

The forum also opens a wider horizon. As AI systems grow in capability and societal integration, questions of long-term coexistence between artificial and human intelligence demand the same rigour we bring to ecological resilience. This session will dedicate space to that deeper inquiry — examining alignment, civilisational sustainability, and the philosophical stakes of the AI transition — informed by scholars from computer science, philosophy, and the humanities.

Thematic Scope

Track A: Green AI — Topics Covered

The session addresses AI's environmental footprint end-to-end, from the physics of computation to global governance frameworks. Contributions are welcome across the following areas:

Full life-cycle assessment of carbon, water, and thermal footprints associated with large-model training and inference
Efficient model architectures: sparse computation, quantisation, knowledge distillation, and the strategic case for smaller models
Software-layer efficiency: green programming languages, compiler optimisation, and intelligent workload scheduling
Environmental impact metrics and reporting standards for AI algorithms — towards internationally comparable disclosure frameworks
Water–energy nexus: water footprint and regional water stress analysis of data centre cooling technologies (air, liquid, and natural cooling)
Waste heat and the urban heat island: contribution of dense compute infrastructure and pathways for waste heat recovery and reuse
Circular economy for AI hardware: sustainable chip and server manufacturing, electronic waste management, and hardware longevity
Renewable energy co-planning: strategic siting of data centres in relation to renewable energy availability and grid constraints
Green AI certification: design and implementation of energy efficiency grading systems, standardised labelling, and regulatory frameworks
Carbon border adjustments and compliance: implications of emerging carbon pricing mechanisms for cross-border AI infrastructure operations
Equity and environmental justice: fairness in AI compute allocation, the global digital divide, and the disproportionate environmental burden on host communities
Corporate transparency and accountability: voluntary and mandatory disclosure of AI environmental impact, and the role of investor and regulatory pressure

Deliverables

Expected Outcomes

GAEA 2026 is designed to generate tangible, lasting outputs beyond the meeting itself.

01
Special Issue — ESE Journal
A curated special issue in Environmental Science and Ecotechnology will publish selected full papers from GAEA 2026, with invited review articles from leading contributors.
02
Science-Policy Brief
A consensus policy brief will be submitted to relevant national authorities, recommending an expansion of data centre efficiency frameworks from PUE alone to an integrated set of metrics encompassing CUE (carbon utilisation efficiency) and WUE (water usage effectiveness).
03
Green AI Shenzhen Declaration
An industry declaration committing signatories to voluntary targets on environmental impact transparency, energy efficiency improvement, and renewable energy adoption. The Declaration is proposed as a candidate deliverable for APEC 2026 (subject to confirmation).
